Meeting the 2015 BMW M3 and M4

In the passenger seat and under the skin of the BMW's most important M cars.

By Chris Cantle September 26, 2013 / Photos by BMW

Slideshow: BMW M3 and M4 Prototypes

Timo Glock’s BMW M3 prototype is eventually destined for the crusher. It’ll live a short, hard life, packed full of suspension experimentation, unusual intercooler setups, tire trials and body-panel tweaks. An opportunist, Glock isn't inclined to leave much car for the scrapyard. That’s how we came to be flinging cones out of our path with gusto and loud, splitter-wrecking smacks, all the while reeling in BMW DTM teammate Andy Priaulx on a tight and technical stretch of BMW’s Maisach training center, just outside of Munich.

The power? The agility? The sound? Brilliant. And ex-F1 driver Glock coaxes the prototype to give us more. He stays on the throttle at a fast right, sliding the car to the edge of the track—and then a little further. We’re in the neighborhood of the 7500 rpm redline, twin turbos spinning hard and coercing the last bit of power out of the 3.0-liter straight-six. Gravel explodes behind us as our left rear leaves the pavement, but we’ve already found traction with the right and the car thrusts forward, chasing Priaulx almost magnetically.


Tantalizingly close to the wheel as I might be, I'm not allowed to helm these prototypes. I'm in Germany for a briefing on the technology that will underpin the new BMW M3 and M4. The ride-along is just to whet everyone's appetites—an exciting two minutes in a day otherwise densely packed with information and 3D renderings.

For the last six months, earnest BMW fanatics have played games of Will it? Won’t it? Today, we get some answers. The frenzy of rumor and conjecture that surrounds BMW’s M3 and M4 might just be what brought an international pack of journalists to Munich in the first place. A little clarity at the hands of BMW’s DTM drivers and the top engineers at the M division will only further build excitement for the F80 and F82 generation of M3 and M4.

Ultimately, the biggest question about the car won’t fade in the light of velocity achieved or corners straightened by BMW employees, regardless of their credentials or enthusiasm for the M3’s apparently abundant front-end grip and feel. The quality of BMW’s M3 and M4 electronic power steering system has been teased and talked about all day, but not quite addressed. Everything else though—power, weight, styling, packaging—that’s all come into focus.

The locals call it Oktoberfest weather. Three M3 and M4 prototypes, now well flogged, radiate their heat on an already hot Upper Bavarian day. Black on white spirals printed onto long strips of vinyl cover the car from the window sills downward. Plastic lips and edges, screwed directly into the body of the car, hide the eventual shapes of air inlets, cowls and wings. These prototypes were working last Friday and will go back to work on Monday, validating all the ideas and design that BMW’s M division is throwing at them. Today, they’re here to shut us up.

Michael Wimbeck knows he has a crowd pleaser on his hands. He’s the guy responsible for telling us that the target weight of the F80 and F82 generation M cars wasn’t just the weight of the current V8-powered E90 M3, but the weight of the previous-generation E46. That makes for a 3300-lb. car in Europe. For the American market, with its weightier options and packages, that would indicate a weight of around 3500 lbs. Maybe less.

The campaign to find and reduce weight sounds relentless. 11 lbs. recovered in the switch from a steel driveshaft to carbon. Two lbs. saved with a magnesium oil sump. 4 lbs. were found in the crankcase and another dozen in the roof. Pistons, decklids, axle carriers and strut braces all got the lightening treatment. The numbers roll on endlessly, but take away this point: The new car is lighter than the old car.

It’s more powerful too. Gone is the delightful but underperforming V8, in favor of a direct-injected twin-turbocharged inline-six. With dry-sump oiling, variable valve timing and lift, as well as those lightweight pistons and a forged crankshaft, it’s all M division, developed just for this car at M’s Preußenstraße facility in Munich. The car nets a solid 17 more promised horsepower, but more importantly, a substantial jump in torque.

Subject of much breathless speculation earlier in the summer, the transmission choices for either car are good and better: BMW’s dual clutch paddle actuated automatic or a six-speed manual pinched from the 1M Coupe and subsequently improved upon. Power and the transmission of power will not be the undoing of this car.

“If we say we have 430 horsepower, we have 430 horses. Altitude, weather, heat. 430. We call that Upper Bavarian Horsepower.” That’s how Albert Biermann, head of M Engineering, sees things from underneath an M4. We’ve spent the better part of a half hour following the path air will travel under the car and discovering the nine heat exchangers it’ll cool. Now we’re getting into philosophy. The turbos don’t typically operate at all 18+ psi, he explains. The new M cars are designed to deliver the performance they’re intended to deliver, wherever the car is delivered. Sea level, the Sierras. Hot or cold, Biermann thinks everyone who buys an M4 deserves the whole experience. Likewise, the M4 will seldom demand the most from the radiators and coolers so intricately arranged across the front of the car, hidden behind fascia and NACA ducts. But they’re there.

“The parts that really matter? They’re all new.” Biermann is pointing into the front wheel well now. He’s right. Where there was once steel, there’s now aluminum. Most pieces bear small Ms. Even the front axle carrier is aluminum. The rear is witness to more changes. Instead of mounting the rear axle carrier comfortably in bushings and quieting the car, the whole operation is bolted directly to the chassis, a change Biermann says sacrifices some harshness for a gain in feeling and directness. It’s not a move that they’d be able to get away with on a common 3 Series.

At the front of the car again, we’re looking right at the bandit responsible for our impatience: the electric power steering motor. It’s innocuous. Small, actually. BMW’s engineers have been patient with us, our questions, our concerns. They have concerns too. As much as they’re willing to explain that they’ve got the steering nailed, finally, through a combination of existing parts, careful calibration and consideration, they will only go that far. We’re going to have to wait out a production car to really know if the integration of EPS in BMW’s performance models can succeed where so many systems have left us a little flat.
